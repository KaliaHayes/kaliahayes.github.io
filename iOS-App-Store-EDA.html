iOS App Store Exploratory Data Analysis

My Business Analytics course included a semseter long group project that involved using Python analytical & visualization libraries and tools to perform an exploratory data analysis on a chosen dataset. My group and I browsed Kaggle.com for datasets and inspiration and eventually settled on a dataset contianing over 7200 iOS mobile application details such as name, size, price, and ratings.

My group selected the Apple App Store dataset for a variety of reasons, with the primary one being familiarity to the topic. We all interact with app stores frequently and are familiar with the front facing aspects involved. As business majors with marketing backgrounds and as consumers who often ask ourselves if a purchase is “worth it,” the idea of analyzing one of the most popular marketplaces was very attractive.

While browsing the dataset, my group determined 4 key questions we believe our data could answer. View our final project writeup or Kaggle notebook for a detailed look at our analysis. Continue below to view a summary of our analysis, download our analysis documentation, our view our Kaggle Python project notebook.

How do you create a best seller mobile app?

To answer this, we analyzed the data to find apps with the highest ratings, then delved deeper and analyzed how elements of those apps contribute to positive consumer sentiment. The elements include but are not limited to: app category, app listings such as descriptions and preview media, app size, and app prices.


Key Findings

After pulling out the top 100 most rated apps we determined those with an average user rating of 4+ stars to be best selling apps. Out of 68 best selling apps in our dataset, 59 free best selling apps and only 9 paid, which means that a majority of the highly sought after apps are in fact free to use. Graph 1 below shows the distribution of best selling app prices, ranging from $0.00 to $6.99. We also determined that best selling apps are usually medium in size, with an average size of ~275mb, as shown in Graph 2 above.

Are paid apps worth the money?

We ask ourselves this question all the time when it comes to making purchases. In order to answer this question, we analyzed the consumer ratings associated with apps of the same category, but differentiated between unpaid and paid apps. Along with this, determined if there are differences between how consumers perceive free apps vs. paid apps.


Key Findings

The average price of all paid apps is $3.96 but may be skewed by possible outliers. We found that the app with the highest price is actually a 4GB Japanese weather app priced at $299.99. Regardless of app price, we discovered that the the average consumer rating of paid apps is 3.72/5 stars. When compared to paid apps within our dataset, the 3.38 average ratings of the 4,057 free apps is lower than that of paid apps. With this, it can be determined that paid apps are “worth it,” if user ratings have anything to say for it. As we can see from Graph 3 & 4 below, the ratings of paid apps are slightly more negatively skewed due to a higher average user rating compared to free apps

What are the leading categories of mobile apps?

We wanted to analyze app genre statistics in the case that one of us decides to create a mobile application one day. Information from our analysis can help us determine the best genre of apps to make. My group and I looked closely at the distribution of app categories to answer this question. We also examined price distributions among paid apps per category using descriptive statistics.


Key Findings

We learned that the leading genre in terms of total apps in our dataset is Games, with a total of 3862 apps. The top five app genres are Games, Entertainment, Education, Photo & Video, and Utilities respectively. Catalogs has the least at only 10. The apps in the Medical, Business, Reference, Music, and Productivity genres all have the highest average app prices, as shown in Graph 5.

Next, we found the leading genre of apps based on user ratings. As it turns out, the leading category in terms of highest average rating would be Productivity with an average rating of 4.01, which is shown in Graph 6 below. Apps in the Productivity genre are generally free of cost, however, those that are paid generally range from $0 - $10.

What size are mobile applications?

Applications come in all sizes, but mobile phones typically have 1TB in storage or less. We've learned that mobile application attributes can make or break and apps' sales, especially application size. To answer this question, we analyzed the file size distribution of apps.


Key Findings

The average size of all apps in our dataset is ~200mb. The first quartile of app size is ~47mb, the second quartile is ~97mb, and the third quartile is ~181mb. The interquartile range thus is ~134mb. Because the mean app size is larger than the median, we know that our data is positively skewed as a result of outliers. Graph 7 below depicts the positively skewed app size distribution data. To get to these answers, we did have to convert bytes to megabytes.

Lastly, we analyzed the average app sizes per genre for free and paid apps. As we can see in Graph 8 & 9, paid apps of the medical, book, and games genre are usually the largest in size. Free apps of the games, medical, and education genre are usually the largest in size.

Career Landing Page

For the career landing page, I wanted to create a space that highlights company perks, programs, and features. After auditing various career pages around the web, I decided to create new web pages highlighting Interstate's company culture, internship program, benefits, and military hiring initiative.

My first plan of attack when designing the new career page was to move the "Apply Now" button towards to top of the viewport in order to draw more attention to it. Also, given that the landing page menu links to those additional career related pages, I chose to offer a brief preview of the content found on each newly created page using pre-existing page building components found within Sitecore in order to match my page style with the overarching website style. Lastly, I decided to include a rotating carousel of quotes from current company employees as a way to make Interstate's culture more transparent to potential candidates in a word-of-mouth style.

Life at Interstate

On the Life At Interstate web page, I really wanted to emphasize the empowering, team based company culture while also giving a holistic view of departments and business units found at Interstate. I reckoned that the page content depicting Life @ Interstate would be a worthy pull-factor in and of itself and would generate job applicants.

Starting at the top, I implemented an immediate call to action by telling viewers to "Join Our Interstate Team". Right below the CTA, I gave a concise yet to the point overview of business units found at Interstate, so that potential applicants can gauge just which team they'd like to join.

The next section of the page detailed Interstate's commitment to fostering a culture when your most impactful self can shine. As an intern, I was highly impressed with the company offered courses, programs, and other career enhancing resources and was eager to show the world a sneak peek.

The last section on the page highlighted just how "fun" working at Interstate could be. I wanted to introduce applicants to the exciting recurring company-wide events.

Internships
